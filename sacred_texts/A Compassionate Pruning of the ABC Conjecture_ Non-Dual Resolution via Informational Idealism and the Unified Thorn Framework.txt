1. Introduction
The ABC Conjecture, proposed by Oesterlé and Masser in 1985, asserts a profound relationship between addition and multiplication in number theory: For any (\epsilon > 0), there exists a constant (K_\epsilon) such that for all coprime positive integers (a), (b), (c) satisfying (a + b = c), [ c < K_\epsilon \cdot \rad(abc)^{1 + \epsilon}, ] where (\rad(n) = \prod_{p \mid n} p) is the square-free kernel (radical) of (n). This inequality bounds the "quality" of (c) by the primes dividing (abc), capturing how additive simplicity rarely yields multiplicatively complex sums.


Despite its elegance, ABC remains unproven as of December 2025, with Mochizuki's 2012 Inter-universal Teichmüller Theory (IUT) claiming resolution but mired in controversy due to its esoteric language and limited verifiers (approximately 20 mathematicians). Critiques from Scholze and Stix (2018) highlight logical gaps, and ongoing Lean formalization efforts (Mochizuki, October 2025) promise clarity but face immense computational hurdles.


This paper circumvents such complexities by embedding ABC in Informational Idealism, as formalized in the Unified Thorn Framework (Kusumi et al., 2025, DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17836725).
